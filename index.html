Assessing Green Bond Yield Differences

Employed a one-to‑one matching algorithm (Hungarian method) to pair 100 green bonds with conventional counterparts on maturity, coupon, issuer and issuance timing, after filtering out callable and supranational bonds to avoid optionality and benchmark distortions. This quasi‑experimental setup minimises omitted‑variable bias and isolates the “greenium” effect on yield to maturity (YTM) :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}. Robustness checks included panel regressions with issuer fixed effects and macro controls (Euribor, Bund yield, VIX), which largely attenuated the greenium, highlighting sensitivity to model specification. Finally, Nelson–Siegel–Svensson curve estimates for both bond groups revealed only marginal term‑structure divergences, suggesting liquidity and characteristic differences drive much of the observed spread.

Sunlight Synchronisation: DST & Emissions

Analysed Australian electricity CO₂ emissions and consumption under heterogeneous Daylight Saving Time (DST) adoption using a Difference-in‑Differences‑in‑Differences (DDD) framework to control for state‑specific shocks and confounders :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}​:contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}. Leveraging an event‑study design across multiple years, we tested the robustness of the parallel‑trends assumption via pre‑trends checks and external control groups. Results indicated a small, statistically insignificant reduction in emissions and consumption, with recent solar uptake amplifying DST’s environmental impact—motivating a deeper focus on post‑2015 solar penetration. Extreme Risk Analysis of FX Markets

Studied USD–EUR daily log‑returns (2004–2023) using Peaks‑Over‑Threshold EVT: fitted Generalised Pareto MLE, then switched to Hill estimator when γ > 0 for efficiency, adjusting CIs for serial dependence :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}. Extrapolated 99.5% and 99.98% quantiles, revealing underestimation by empirical maxima and underscoring the need for systematic tail modeling in capital reserve calculations. Graphical diagnostics (moment and Q‑Q plots) confirmed heavy tails; additional intra‑day high analysis via Weibull fits highlighted even greater tail risk. Findings inform hedging strategies and regulatory stress‑testing for currency crises.

Scoring & Default Prediction

Drawing on roughly 600 000 firm‑year observations from WRDS Compustat and CRSP covering the period 1978‑2023, I built a complete credit‑risk‑prediction pipeline in R, relying on the tidyverse for data wrangling and glmnet, randomForest, and pROC for modelling and evaluation.

The first stage focused on data engineering: extreme financial ratios were winsorised, all infinite or missing values were imputed, and gvkey–permno links were reconciled to yield a clean, time‑ordered panel suitable for rolling‑window splits.

As a benchmark I replicated the Altman Z‑score with a plain logistic regression, which produced an AUC of about 0.72. Because defaults are rare, I then applied inverse‑frequency class weights; this single adjustment cut false‑negative errors by a factor of six and lifted the AUC to 0.89. Pushing sparsity further, a weighted LASSO retained only five core financial ratios yet still preserved an AUC of 0.90.

To capture non‑linear effects I trained a 500‑tree Random Forest. Its class‑weighted variant delivered the strongest hold‑out result, with an AUC close to 0.94 and only eleven missed bankruptcies out of 106.

Realistic performance was assessed with five‑fold time‑series cross‑validation. Under this stricter regime Altman‑style models dropped to AUC 0.61, and even a Box–Cox‑transformed Altman specification slipped to  0.54—evidence of look‑ahead bias in static splits. Finally, I implemented structural Distance‑to‑Default estimators (Schumway 2001 and an iterative Merton approach); both hovered near random, at AUC ≈ 0.51, underscoring the difficulty of market‑based models when applied to accounting‑heavy data.

In sum, the combination of class weighting and ensemble trees provided the most dependable balance between recall and interpretability for highly imbalanced default data.
